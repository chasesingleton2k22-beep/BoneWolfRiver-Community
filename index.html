Orbus the Bereft — Complete Mythology
OriginAt the beginning there were thirteen godlike beings. One father. Twelve sons. Orbus was the thirteenth. His nature was chaos — not by choice but by what he was. He sowed madness among his brothers. He turned his chaos toward his father and would have killed him. His brothers and father combined and sealed him inside a comet and sent him here. He did not choose to come to Earth. He was thrown here.
ArrivalHe arrived with the Younger Dryas comet impact approximately 12,900 years ago. The impact nearly destroyed Earth. Ended the ice age. Killed the megafauna. Destroyed civilizations that existed before recorded history. When he arrived he was still strong. He bent the survivors to his will. Demanded the blood of their brightest children.
What He Is NowWeakened over thirteen thousand years. He hides in the seams of things. The edges of firelight. The dark between stars. He lies dormant for centuries and surfaces when the chosen one’s flame burns bright enough to pull him up.
His CycleWhen he surfaces he feeds on the Kindled — the bright-blooded — through the Fenton bloodline. Gets to functional strength. Then focuses entirely on the chosen one. Tries to inhabit them completely. Has failed twice. This is the third attempt.
His MotiveHe believes if he inhabits the chosen one completely he can use their power to nudge a comet — NASA confirms a tiny push is all it takes — and the impact will thin the seams between this world and his father’s world enough for him to step through and go home. Whether Earth survives is not his concern.
What the Legends SayThe ancient traditions — including the medicine woman’s — say yes the comet thins the seams. But what steps through is not a god going home. It is something coming in. Orbus has never addressed this. He only knows what he knows.
His NatureHe is the Bereft. Not because he chose evil. Because thirteen thousand years of isolation and hunger and diminishment has made him monstrous. He was cast out because his nature was chaos. The isolation turned the chaos inward. He is the man on the street corner with newspapers taped to his body counting things in the air that nobody else can see. Completely certain. Completely alone. Completely lost. He was not always this.
The DelusionHe has been sitting in the dark for thirteen thousand years telling himself a story. A reason. A purpose. He believes if he gets home his brothers and his father will be waiting. That the impact will thin the seams. That something will be on the other side. The reader gradually sees what Anthony and Nicole cannot yet see — the twelve gods are not waiting for him. The way home may not exist the way he believes it does. He does not consider this. He has never considered this. He only knows what he knows. And what he knows is the story he has been telling himself in the dark for thirteen thousand years.
His AppearanceFeatureless. Plain. A face the eye cannot hold. Nothing to name, nothing to keep. Only the eyes are distinct — burning, half filled with sorrow, half filled with something older than hate. A crown of light appears above his head near the gifted. Not a symbol of power. A wound that never healed taking the shape of what he once thought he deserved. He is the king of madness and loneliness.
As He FeedsHis featureless face flickers. Not becoming something. Just flickering like a candle in wind. In those flickers — faces. Young faces. Adolescents. Children. Even babies. The faces of everyone he has ever consumed passing across his featureless face like reflections on disturbed water. He is not haunted by them. They are simply part of what he is now. Cole Fenton sees this when he is close enough. Sometimes in the flickering he sees faces he recognizes.
Ancient DepictionsThe Urfa Man — oldest life-size statue ever found, 9000 BC, no mouth, black obsidian eyes. Found near Göbekli Tepe. The survivors made a statue of him. The Sphinx — original form was a wolf, built during the wet Sahara period after his arrival, facing east. Pillar 43 at Göbekli Tepe — the vulture stone — depicts the Younger Dryas comet impact date. Other pillars depict a featureless figure holding a child. The wolf stone depicts a wolf-man figure. In the ancient traditions he is sometimes called the Lonely Wolf — the figure that moves through the dark between the fires.

The Book — The Self-Writing Record
OriginMade by the medicine man at Göbekli Tepe approximately 9600 BC. He was the most holy person his people had ever produced. He spent three days fashioning it from a fragment of the comet that brought Orbus — black obsidian on one side, dried skin on the other, bound with sinew cord. The impact debris still faintly warm, humming at a frequency felt in the back teeth. Animals would not approach it. He wove his medicine into the binding using rituals that died with him. When it was complete he breathed white light from his body into the front cover and pressed his hands in. His skin burned and sizzled. He did not stop. No living tradition knows what he did those three days. Only what he made.
What It DoesThe book writes itself. It records the chosen ones — their story, what happened to them, what Orbus did, what it cost — in the language of each era it records. Nobody writes in it. The entries simply appear. Each section in the language closest to the chosen one it describes.
How It MovesThe book moves to the chosen one when the time comes. The medicine woman’s tradition — a line of medicine women stretching back to the survivors of the Younger Dryas — carries it forward. Grandmother to granddaughter. With specific instructions: when the comet burns in October, find where the fire is. Get there before the other one does. The hibachi chef is the current medicine woman in this line. She sent the book to the household after seeing Carter put his hands in the flame. She was recognizing, not discovering.
How It HelpsIt shows the chosen one what came before. Every chosen one who ever lived. Their story already written. So they know they are not the first. So they know the pattern. So they know what Orbus is doing before he finishes doing it.
ArrivalThe book arrived addressed to Carter. Carter is eighteen months old. The hibachi chef — a medicine woman whose tradition has known about the book for generations — found it and sent it to the household when she saw Carter put his hands in the flame and the crown appeared above his shadow. She knew the time had come.
ContentsApproximately thirteen sections. Each in a different language. The front section is Göbekli Tepe proto-script — the oldest written language on Earth. Nicole eventually identifies it and gets partial translation through an archaeologist contact. Middle sections in various ancient and medieval languages. One section in archaic English that Anthony can barely parse — this is Michaela Marie’s section, the one he finds when he flips through at midnight. The last pages — mostly blank. In the bottom corner of the final page: Carter’s name and birthday. October 13 2024. Written in no hand that belongs to anyone.
Ancient Sites — The Survivors’ Monuments
Göbekli Tepe — 9600–8200 BCSoutheastern Turkey. The oldest temple ever found. Pre-dates any other megalithic site by 6,000 years. Deliberately buried by its builders. T-shaped pillars with animal carvings. Pillar 43 — the vulture stone — encodes the date of the Younger Dryas impact as a star map. The first written word found here means God. LIDAR has detected 15–20 additional structures beneath the hill not yet excavated. One pillar depicts a featureless figure holding a child. One pillar has a wolf carved into it. The survivors built this to warn whoever came after them.
Karahan Tepe — Same Period45 kilometers east of Göbekli Tepe. Sister site. 250 obelisks. A human face carved emerging from bedrock on a serpent-like neck. Deliberately buried. LIDAR revealing T-shaped pillars inside a ritual well. In 1999 during early excavations something was disturbed. The Fenton bloodline activity in Nicole’s research spikes noticeably in the early 2000s.
The Urfa Man — 9000 BCFound near Göbekli Tepe. Oldest life-size human statue ever found. No mouth. Eyes originally emphasized by black obsidian sunk into deep holes. The obsidian comes from a geological formation buried under the Younger Dryas impact debris. The survivors used a piece of what brought him here to make his eyes.
The Sphinx — 10,000–12,000 BCBuilt during the wet Sahara period after the impact. Original form was a wolf — not a lion, not a human face. The head was recarved by later civilizations who found it and didn’t understand what they were looking at. Faces east. Watching the sky where the comet came from. Dr. Robert Schoch’s water erosion evidence dates it to this period.
Tell Qaramel — Syria — 10,500 BCFive stone towers. The earliest structures built by the survivors. Before the full theology developed. Just towers watching the sky.

He holds the full weight of thirteen thousand years of Orbus’s grief and loneliness and does not break. And in that moment — Carter’s eyes flash ice blue. The father’s light. The cold between stars. Waiting in the bloodline for the one who could hold it.

Register & Sentence-Level Rules
Never the same noun twice for the same thing in close proximity — vary words for people, groups, the dead, time, violence.

Groups: souls, hands, riders, the company, the congregation, that gathering, the host, the assembled, the multitude, the column, the procession, the camp, the nation, the silent.
Single person: the rider, that figure, the one, he who, this man, the stranger, the witness, that soul, the elder.
Violence/killing: fell, went down, was taken, was undone, ceased, was ended, met his end, went still, was claimed, was finished, came apart, was unmade.
Time passing: in that hour, by the time, before the light failed, as the dark came on, when the sun stood at its height, ere long, presently, in the after.
Use sparingly but deliberately — one or two per page, not every sentence. Overuse turns gravity into parody.

“about” for “around” · “without” meaning outside of · “ere” for “before” · “whence” for “from where” · “save” for “except” · “did” + verb for emphasis (“He did not speak. He did wait.”) · “that” for “so that”.
Stack clauses the way Old Testament genealogies stack clauses — accumulating weight through repetition of structure, not new information.

“And the riders came on, and the dust rose up behind them, and the horses’ breath smoked in the cold, and none among them spoke.”

Use for arrival, gathering dread, ceremony. Never for fast action — it slows the reader on purpose.
After any act of violence, drop into flat, unadorned statement. No metaphor, no elevated language — the contrast is the device.

“The knife went into him and he sat down in the dirt and that was all.”

Do not follow violence with more violence-description or metaphor stacking. Land the blow, go flat and short. The quiet after is louder than more noise would be.
Join clauses with “and” instead of commas or subordinating conjunctions — relentless, biblical, unstoppable.

“And he rose and he took up the blade and he went out into the dark and the wolf went with him and neither of them looked back.”

Use for: rising action, momentum toward an inevitable event, ceremony, fate closing in. Avoid for: quiet domestic scenes, anything that needs to breathe.
Entering darkness, fog, smoke, or any obscured space: give smell or sound BEFORE sight. Order — smell, sound, vague shape, full sight, detail. Never skip straight to visual detail.

“Smoke first. Then the low murmur of a camp that did not know yet that it had visitors. Then, after a moment, the shapes of the lodges resolving out of the dark.”
A sound is heard before the thing making it is seen or understood — builds dread without exposition.

“A sound came from the treeline, low and sustained, and it was a long moment before any among them understood it for a man’s voice.”

Use constantly for the wolf, for Orbus’s approach, for any threat.
Tags carry who spoke AND how they inhabit the words: pondered, allowed, offered, considered. Invert verb-before-name for weight — “pondered Blood On His Claws” not “Blood On His Claws pondered.” Use neutral “said” when neutral is correct; a loaded verb when the character’s relationship to their own words matters.
Character & Body Rules
Every character introduced gets: (1) a PAST wound or mark on the body, described from inside it — not “he had a scar” but the texture of what it cost him; (2) a FUTURE hint woven in — “dead within the month,” “never see this face again,” “that scar would outlast the war.”
Before finishing any scene, confirm: what is this person doing with their body right now? Where are their hands? What did they just set down? Where are their eyes? How are they sitting? Every person in a scene needs at least one physical gesture, placement, or stillness — even if they don’t speak.
Never “looked at” when a stronger verb exists: regarded, studied, considered, watched, fixed his eyes on, held his gaze on.

Not: “He looked at the knife for a long moment.”
Instead: “He studied the knife for a long moment.” or “He fixed his eyes on the knife and did not move.”
The known tendency: hitting the moment and then adding one line too many. After any emotionally or mythically loaded line, stop — don’t explain the image, don’t reinforce the feeling a second way. Self-check: did I just say this twice in two different ways? If yes, cut the second one.
Scene-Level Rules
Periodically break from the main scene for a short, self-contained image or story that seems unrelated but deepens the world. Use 1–2 per chapter MAXIMUM. Never explain themselves, never connect overtly to the plot — their job is atmosphere and scale.

Example: a warband’s aftermath glimpsed on the trail and ridden past without comment. A burned homestead that isn’t Knox’s. A stranger at a fire who speaks once and is never named again.
Direct scenes show what a character does; secondhand testimony makes a character into legend. McCarthy’s Tobin tells the kid about the judge around the judge’s own direct scenes — the judge is terrifying ON the page, mythic because of what Tobin says OFF it.

For this book: a recurring secondary witness (old scout/tracker) who tells newer warriors what he’s seen Katawa do, 3–4 times across the Carter chapters, always at a fire, always to someone who wasn’t there. Half reverence, half warning. One defining physical detail, one defining piece of history — no full arc needed.

“I seen him walk out of that fire myself. Skin clean. Not a mark on him. You don’t believe me, you ask anybody who was there that night.”

Dark scenes get dark sky description. Good or neutral scenes get clean sky. Weather always mirrors the scene’s moral temperature — never describe sky neutrally when the scene has weight.

Dark beat: “The sky over the camp had gone the color of a bruise, and no star showed through it.”
Good/neutral beat: “The morning came up clean and blue and without complaint.”
For high-stakes turning points — death, defeat, transformation — structure in four beats:

1. Real damage first. Something actually breaks — the body, a weapon, a plan. Not symbolic, literal.
2. A vision or memory at the threshold. While hurt or overwhelmed, the character drifts — sees someone they love, a past moment, a voice.
3. A corruption or twist arriving through the most sacred thing they carry. The vision turns wrong — the loved voice says something it never would, the safe memory curdles.
4. Return changed. They come back, but not clean — costly or ambiguous, never a clean triumph.

Worked example (already in the book): the Wolf Circle duel in Chapter Six. (1) Spotted Horse’s blow lands — real damage. (2) Kata staggers toward the circle’s edge, near death. (3) Roxy’s voice arrives — twisted by Orbus, saying “Burn bright, my fire… they all shall burn,” the most sacred voice he has, corrupted. (4) He gives way and wins — but costly: an innocent boy loses an eye, and something in Kata has been used by something else.
Every transition — a ride, a walk, a drive — is an opportunity, not dead space. Before writing any travel passage, pick ONE job for it:

· Character revelation through small action — how someone packs a bag, holds the reins, tells you who they are.
· A tension beat disguised as nothing — two people talking about the weather while something unsaid sits between them.
· The thing two people haven’t said yet — silence doing the work dialogue would normally do.
· Mythology delivered as sensation — the world’s history felt in passing (a burned-out homestead glimpsed, not explained) rather than info-dumped.

Weak (dead space): “They rode for three days. The land was flat and dry.”
Working (tension beat disguised as nothing): “They rode three days and spoke only of the horses. Neither one said her name. By the second night the not-saying-it had become its own kind of company.”
For a STATE of the world (terrain, ruins, settled objects) rather than a one-time action, switch to present tense even inside past-tense narration — gives a timeless, ongoing, landscape-fact quality.

“A ladder yet leans against the side of the house” (not “had been leaned”). “A fence runs along the property line” (not “ran”). “The river bends hard to the east just past the ridge.”

People act in past tense. The land simply IS, in present tense, even in a past-tense scene.

Voice & Structure — McCarthy / GRRM Extensions
McCarthy almost never states what a character feels. Show the gesture, the stillness, the thing the hands do — never write “she felt a wave of grief.” If the body presence rule (11) is followed correctly, naming the emotion becomes redundant and should be cut.

Not: “He felt a cold dread settle over him.”
Instead: “His hand stopped on the latch. He did not open the door.”
Zoom a moment out against the age of stone, the indifference of stars, things older than men. Use sparingly, at the heaviest beats — this is a high-value tool given Orbus is literally ancient and cosmic in origin.

“The stars overhead did not care, had not cared in all the long turning of the world, and would not begin now.”
Periodically let one character deliver a long, unsettling, philosophically loaded speech that reframes the book’s morality — McCarthy’s judge in Blood Meridian. Orbus and Blood On His Claws are the natural vessels for this in this book. Use rarely — 1–2 times across the whole manuscript, never as small talk.
Be exact about where the blade goes, what organ, what angle — not impressionistic. This is the strike itself, distinct from rule 4 (the flat statement that follows it).

Not: “The knife appeared in his neck.”
Instead: “The blade went in below the jaw and found the artery and did not need to go any further.”
Resist clean emotional resolution at chapter and arc endings. No tears-and-closure unless the moment has earned ambiguity instead. The reader should often be left with weight, not relief.

Cathartic (avoid): “He wept for his mother, and in that release he finally let her go, and walked forward lighter than he had in years.”
Non-cathartic (use instead): “He did not weep. He stood over the grave a while longer than he meant to, then turned and walked back the way he’d come, and nothing in him felt any different at all.”
A chapter never knows more than its POV character knows — GRRM’s rule. With this book braiding modern Carter, historical Carter, Ten Bears, and others, no chapter should reveal information, motive, or fact that its POV character has no way of knowing yet.
Give antagonists real ability and real, understandable ambition before — or instead of — making them monstrous. Blood On His Claws already follows this (see his Character Arcs card); extract it here as a reusable rule for future antagonists.
GRRM’s prophecies mislead by being technically true, not by being vague. Write the line so it is 100% accurate once you know the answer — but the reader (and the character) should naturally assume it means something else first.

Worked example (already in the book): Orbus calls Carter “my herald, my promise.” Read at face value, this sounds like Carter is Orbus’s champion — something to fear. The technically-true reading, recontextualized later, could instead mean Carter is the herald of Orbus’s ending, the promise of his defeat. Same words, opposite meaning, both fully supported by the text.

How to write one: decide the real, final meaning first. Then write the line so a reader assumes the obvious-but-wrong meaning, using words exact enough that you never have to retcon or contradict it later.
No plot armor, no slow-motion sendoff. Named characters can die mid-scene, without warning. Relevant directly to Ten Bears’ murder — resist over-narrating it into a Big Sad Moment. Let it be sudden and brutal, the grief processed after, not during.
Plant a detail chapters — or eras — ahead of its payoff. This book is structurally built for it: objects, melodies, and images crossing from 1854 to 2026 (see the Echo Map in Story Compass). Treat every cross-era echo as a standing structural tool, not a one-off device.

Character logic, not sentence-level craft — use when building or revising a character.

1. No villain in his own mind. Every character, Cole Fenton included, needs internal justification that makes sense from inside his own head.
2. Plant before payoff, quietly. A small detail dropped early without emphasis, paying off chapters later. The reader shouldn’t clock it on first pass.
3. One contradicting trait per major character. A trait that cuts against the archetype — the warrior gentle with children, the elder petty about something small.
4. Cost without warning. The hardest losses land harder with no foreshadowing dirge, no lingering goodbye scene. Roxy’s death already does this well — protect that instinct elsewhere.
5. Every POV character wants something the plot isn’t about. Give side characters a private want that has nothing to do with Orbus or the bloodline.

Voice, Imagery & Vocabulary Banks
Not just dialogue tags (see rule 15) — apply to any action verb. Instead of “Blood On His Claws said with a smile,” write “Smiled Blood On His Claws.” The verb carries the action; the name arrives after. Apply to stood, turned, watched, spat, growled.
Use words as adjectives that grammar says they shouldn’t be: “advanced elongate before them.” One per chapter maximum — any more becomes a tic. Candidates: prone, elongate, supine, pendulous, canted.
Never say it was a long time. Stack it: “Days of begging. Days of theft. Days of riding where there rode no soul save he.” Use for Katawa’s trials, Ten Bears’ years, the Fenton line, the Keeper’s generations.
In narration, hint at what’s coming: “If he gave thanks to God for what had happened, he was ill-timed in his gratitude, for destiny was not done with him yet.” The narrator knows the future; the character doesn’t. Use sparingly — three or four times in the whole book. More than that becomes a tic.
Describe a landscape once, then return a few sentences later with a changed detail — movement, light, weather, sound. First pass establishes the place; second pass makes it alive. Example: describe the lake, then later — wind ruffling it in streaks of silver. A strength to protect, never cut.
Instead of dark or shadowy, push into something spatial: “The further dim of the house.” “The slow dark beyond the fire.” Darkness as depth, not just description.
Shadows reach, lurk, and foreshadow: “Like tentacles to bind them to the darkness yet to come.” The shadow isn’t just where light isn’t — it’s what’s waiting. Use for Katawa’s shadow acting before he does, the wolf’s shadow appearing before the wolf, Speaks Like The Stars’ shadows reaching toward the boy.
Light moves up surfaces with intention: “Soft light advanced up the barrels of the guns and the harness of the horses.” Light as something traveling toward you. Use at dawn, firelight, lantern glow.
Lightning doesn’t just flash — it shapes and reveals: “The lightning shaped out the distant mountains.” “Brief flashes sketched the valley in palest indigo.” Light as a revealing force, not mere illumination.
When a character watches rings spread in still water, the beauty is not lost on them. Use before or after violence — the perfect geometry of rings against chaos.

Landscape Field Guide — By Region & Month
Read before a scene set in that place and season. All flora is real to these Texas regions.
The Wolves, the Prayer Spot, the Eternal Forest’s upper edge. Volcanic mountains the color of ash, rust, and old bone. Pale stone, canyons fifteen hundred feet deep, the clear cold river a green ribbon through parched rock. Lechuguilla and dagger-leaved agave on the slopes; true pine/oak/madrone forest in the cool heights.

The mountains stood up out of the desert floor like something not yet finished, the rock the color of dried blood and old ash, and turkey vultures rode the thermals above the canyons all the day long on wings that never beat, black shapes tracing slow circles over something already decided. At dusk the great horned owls came on without sound, and a man might stand in the dark and not know one had passed within a hand’s breadth of his skull until he heard, far off, the small thing it had taken. Ravens kept to the rimrock and spoke to one another in a tongue that sounded like men arguing over the dead. Mountain lions moved through the high country, rarely seen and never forgotten by those who saw them; javelina rooted in the lechuguilla at first light, and the black-tailed rattlesnake lay coiled in the cool of the rock through the heat of the day, patient as the stone itself.

Creatures: golden eagle, common raven, great horned owl, turkey vulture, canyon wren (its falling song like something giving up), mountain lion, black bear, javelina, gray fox, black-tailed rattlesnake.

Jan–Feb: Big Bend bluebonnet (tallest of all bluebonnets) begins early, sometimes January; by February whole hillsides go blue — “blue spilled from the sky.” Bicolor mustard carpets the desert floor, sweet-scented, first true sign of spring.
Feb–Mar: Ocotillo throws flaming scarlet tips up to twenty feet — “candles from a candelabra.” Texas mountain laurel blooms purple, smelling of grape, March into May.
Mar–Jun: Ocotillo continues, feeding migrating hummingbirds. Orange Mexican poppies, red firewheels, purple wooly locoweed; purple verbena and pink globe mallow line the arroyos; lemon-yellow desert marigold in the dry washes.
Summer: Barrel cacti crown magenta and lavender. Lechuguilla’s tall yellow blooms. Yellow esperanza bells swarmed by bees. Faxon yucca raises white blooms; rare pink-flowered yuccas in the best years.

Writing note: the sacred high country. Blue bluebonnets and scarlet ocotillo against pale volcanic rock is the signature palette of the Prayer Spot in spring — the father’s country wears blue and flame.
Where the river leaves the mountains. Canyon walls rising sheer, water green and fast in the narrows; gravel bars and reed beds where it opens. Cottonwoods and willows crowd the banks — first real riverside trees.

Belted kingfishers worked the green water in the narrows, falling on minnows like something flung down from above, and great blue herons stood the gravel bars so still they might have been carved there, waiting on the river the way a debt waits on a man. The grass ran to the edge of the world in every direction and the wind never stopped moving through it, and far off a man could mark the passing of riders by the dust before he heard the horses, and hear the horses before he saw a single face among them.
